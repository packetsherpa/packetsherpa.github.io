I shared my story for the first time as the speaker at a meeting last night.&nbsp; I was nervous at first.&nbsp; Who wouldn't be?&nbsp; When you share your story, you're bearing your soul -- to a room full of people that you don't know.

But there's magic in those rooms.&nbsp; The people who fill them understand each other in ways that those on the outside simply do not.&nbsp; Once I got started, I fell into an easy cadence and was able to speak with clarity and confidence.<!--more-->

The response from the group was phenomenal.&nbsp; People heard messages that I didn't even realize I was communicating.&nbsp; They heard that I am a family man, and that I got sober in part for the benefit of my son and my wife.&nbsp; They heard that alcoholism is a slow burning, progressive illness that overtakes us slowly but surely.&nbsp; They heard that I'd found freedom and had gotten the monkey off my back.

I didn't actually say any of those things, but looking back on my story and my words, those messages ring true.

I was delighted that several members of my home group came to see me share, including my sponsor.&nbsp; Their presence helped put me at ease and I was truly grateful that they were there.

I also made some new connections last night -- for those, I'm truly grateful.&nbsp; See, we're all in this together in recovery.&nbsp; We need each other. &nbsp;We can't do it alone.

If there was one word that defined my alcoholism, it is <em>isolation</em>. In my recovery, I've started to break through those walls and rejoin the human race.

Some say that suffering <em>is</em> the human condition.&nbsp; And that is certainly part of it, but I also believe that connection is part of the human condition.&nbsp; Connections with others make us human and help us deal with the suffering.

We all need connections.&nbsp; We cannot live a joyful life alone.
